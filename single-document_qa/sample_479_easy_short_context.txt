ABSTRACT 
The rapid development of battery technologies requires significant time and effort to conduct experiments and obtain battery 
characteristics. To address this challenge, this paper develops an electrochemical-thermal coupled model to simulate batteries, where 
the model parameters will be determined by using voltage and temperature as optimization targets. A genetic algorithm is employed 
for parameter identification and optimization. The model is validated through experiments on various battery cells under different 
working conditions. The results demonstrate that the simulated voltage from the model achieves high accuracy with the root mean 
square error (RMSE) ranging between 16mV and 34mV. Furthermore, a battery lifetime model is also explored by incorporating multiple 
internal degradation mechanisms. Validation results show that the RMSE in lifetime prediction is less than 0.0024. As a result, the 
developed models have achieved high accuracy in battery performance simulation and lifetime prediction with a certain degree of 
generalizability, allowing for quick adaptation to other types of battery cells and thereby reducing testing costs and shortening model 
development cycle.  
KEYWORDS 
Lithium-ion battery, electrochemical model, model calibration, parameter identification, lifetime prediction. 
 
1 Introduction 
ith the continuous development of industrial technology, environmental pollution and energy 
shortages have become increasingly prominent issues[1]. Electric vehicles (EVs) provide an effective 
solution for reducing energy consumption and carbon emissions, contributing to the strategic goals 
of "carbon peaking" and "carbon neutrality"[2,3]. As a result, the widespread promotion of EVs has become a 
pressing priority for driving the sustainable development of the automotive industry. Lithium-ion batteries 
have emerged as the preferred power source for EVs due to their high specific energy, high operating voltage, 
high energy density, long lifespan, low self-discharge rate, and environmental friendliness. However, the 
performance and lifespan of lithium-ion batteries are influenced by various factors, such as battery design, 
material selection, charging and discharging strategies, and operation conditions[4]. Therefore, the 
development of battery simulation optimization techniques and lifespan prediction methods is crucial for 
enhancing the performance and extending the service life of lithium-ion batteries. By creating mathematical 
models and utilizing optimization algorithms, battery simulation methods enable evaluation and 
enhancement of battery performance. This virtual testing approach significantly reduces experimental costs 
and time while increasing the efficiency and accuracy of design processes[5].  
However, due to the complex multi-physics coupling processes within batteries and uncertainties in 
electrochemical reactions and material degradation, battery simulation optimization faces numerous 
challenges[6,7]. The pseudo-two-dimensional (P2D) electrochemical model and its various modifications are 
widely used because they capture the essential electrochemical process within the batteries, including the 
dynamics of ion transport, electrode reactions, and charge/discharge mechanisms[8]. By considering the heat 
generated during electrode reactions, these models evolve into electrochemical-thermal coupling models 
that offer a more comprehensive description of battery behavior, incorporating both reaction kinetics and 
thermodynamics[9,10].

Lifespan prediction is a vital technology for ensuring the safety and reliability of batteries, particularly 
in EVs, where battery performance and safety are of utmost important[22]. Accurate lifespan prediction 
enables users to assess battery reliability, implement appropriate maintenance and management strategies 
to extend battery life[23]. Based on the operating conditions and historical data of the batteries, lifespan 
prediction methods apply degradation models to estimate battery's remaining life[24]. By predicting battery 
lifespan, users gain insights into the aging mechanisms which affect battery performance over time. This 
information allows for the optimization of charging and discharging strategies, reducing the rate of 
degradation and enhancing the overall safety and reliability of the battery system[25]. Battery simulation plays 
a key role in this process by mimicking long-term performance decay and aging processes under various 
conditions, provides valuable data for reliability analysis and more accurate lifespan prediction[26]. 
This paper aims to investigate the simulation optimization and lifespan prediction of lithium-ion 
batteries. Key scientific issues: such as modeling mechanism, parameter identification, and lifespan 
prediction, are fundamental to the study of power batteries. Research in these areas holds significant 
theoretical and practical value, contributing to advancements in battery management technologies and 
supporting the development of EV industries. In this study, an electrochemical-thermal coupled model was 
developed for lithium-ion batteries, with voltage and temperature serving as optimization objectives for 
parameter identification and model refinement. The model was validated using experimental data from 
different battery cells under various operating conditions. Furthermore, an aging model accounting for 
multiple internal degradation mechanisms was established[27]. The validation of both voltage response and 
lifespan prediction simulations demonstrated that the developed models can effectively simulate battery 
voltage and the degradation process with a certain degree of generalizability. This contributes to a deeper 
understanding of battery behavior, allowing for more accurate predictions of battery lifespan and enhancing 
the ability to optimize battery management strategies in real-world applications.

2.2 Experimental procedure 
For the above-mentioned battery cells, four of them were used in the experiments to identify 
inconsistencies stemming from the manufacturing process. Each cell began with capacity test at low 
discharge rate, where the cell at the fully charged state was discharged at a discharge current of C/20 until 
its lower cut-off voltage was reached.  This low discharge rate was employed to prevent activation of the cell's 
dynamics, ensuring that the measured voltage reflected the open-circuit voltage (OCV) of the cell.  
Moreover, the cells were subjected to constant current (CC) discharge tests at various rates: 1C, 1.5C, 2C, 
and 3C, along with Dynamic Stress Test (DST). During each of these tests, the cell was first charged at a CC of 
1C, followed by constant voltage (CV) charging until the current dropped to C/20. Then, the fully charged cell 
was allowed to rest before being discharged to its lower cut-off voltage. Following each discharge, the cell 
was allowed to rest again before the next charge cycle commenced. To monitor capacity degradation over 
time, an additional low discharge rate OCV test was performed for every 100 cycles. 
3 Simulation optimization method 
The methodology framework of this study is depicted in Figure 1. A P2D model was employed to simulate 
the performance of lithium-ion cells, and its parameters were identified to minimize the root mean square 
error (RMSE) between the calculated and experimental voltages and temperatures using a genetic algorithm. 
Subsequently, the RMSE between the calculated and experimental state of health (SOH) was minimized to 
identify the parameters of the cell aging model.  The details are explained as follows.

3.1 Model development

3.1.3 Electrochemical-thermal coupling model 
The performance and aging of lithium-ion cells are highly sensitive to temperature. At low temperatures, 
the diffusion process slows down, reducing cell performance. Under extreme high temperature, aging 
accelerates, and also the cell faces safety risks such as thermal runaway[31]. Therefore, an electrochemical 
model coupled with thermal effects is crucial for predicting cell performance, aging, and safety[32]. 
The electrochemical model allows for the calculation of lithium-ion cell reactions under constant 
temperature conditions. The thermal model can compute heat generation and temperature rise during cell 
operation[33]. Since the reaction rates in the electrochemical system vary with temperature, the core of 
electrochemical-thermal coupling is the adjustment of electrochemical model parameters as temperature 
changes. When the model parameters are adjusted based on temperature, the cell's operating characteristics 
will change, which in turn will lead to the alteration in the rate of heat generation[34]. 
Thus, the electrochemical-thermal coupling model is developed based on the interaction between 
electrochemical reactions and heat generation. By solving the mass, charge, and energy conservation 
equations, along with electrochemical kinetics, the model computes lithium-ion concentrations in both the 
solid and liquid phases, potentials in the solid and liquid phases, the volumetric current density of 
electrochemical reactions, and cell temperature[35]. 
 
3.1.4 Aging model 
The performance of lithium-ion batteries degrades gradually with the increase in charge-discharge 
cycles. The aging of lithium-ion batteries is primarily caused by physical factors such as thermal and 
mechanical stress, as well as chemical factors including side reactions within the cell[36]. The aging 
mechanisms can be classified into two major categories: Loss of Lithium Inventory (LLI) and Loss of Active 
Material (LAM)[37]. LLI is primarily driven by the growth of the Solid Electrolyte Interphase (SEI) layer[38], 
electrolyte decomposition, and lithium plating side reactions, whereas LAM results mainly from the 
exfoliation of graphite in the anode, particle cracking, and the increase in resistance due to corrosion of the 
current collectors. This study considers three aging mechanisms: SEI layer growth on the anode, Cathode 
Electrolyte Interphase (CEI) layer growth on the cathode, and the loss of active material from both 
electrodes[39].

3.2.2 Parameter identification 
Due to the structural complexity of electrochemical models and the large number of parameters in the 
models, a sensitivity analysis of the model parameters was conducted prior to parameter identification. 
Highly sensitive parameters have a significant impact on model output and are thus easier to identify. In 
contrast, low-sensitivity parameters exert little influence on the output, making them difficult to determine 
through parameter identification. By focusing on the identification of highly sensitive parameters, the 
precision and efficiency of the identification process can be significantly improved. The experimental data 
required for parameter identification follow a principle of increasing complexity: starting with the data from 
low current rate to high current rate tests, from normal to extreme temperature tests, and from calendar life 
to cycle life tests.

responses. Optimizing transient response can be viewed as an infinite multi-objective optimization problem,

5 Conclusion 
This paper proposes an optimization method for the calibration of lithium-ion cells based on an 
electrochemical model. A P2D electrochemical-thermal coupling model was used for the cells, considering 
the impact of various aging factors on cell lifespan. Experimental data from different operational conditions 
were utilized to optimize and calibrate the model, with mutual validation performed for models obtained 
from different cells and conditions. 
The simulation results of the parameter calibration for lithium-ion cells demonstrate that the model 
accurately replicates the experimentally measured voltage and temperature curves, with the best RMSE 
values of 14 mV and 0.796Â°C. The aging model achieved an RMSE of only 0.001, indicating high accuracy in 
parameter calibration. The case study results show good cross-validation between the voltage curves of 
different cells, with RMSE values below 34 mV, demonstrating the model's generalizability. The RMSE for 
capacity fade curve validation was below 0.0024, proving the effectiveness of lifespan prediction and the
